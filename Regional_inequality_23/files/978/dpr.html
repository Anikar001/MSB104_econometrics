Summary

Motivation

Do financial crises affect everyone equally? Despite the growing concern about income inequality recently, mixed empirical findings have resulted in a long-standing debate about the distribution of income in the aftermath of financial crises.

Purpose

The study examines how different types of financial crises affect the distribution of income.

Methods and approach

In this study we used a two-step system Generalized method of moments (GMM) model to explore the effects of financial crisis on income inequality.

Findings

Any type of financial crisis results in a larger income gap between the rich and the poor. Although little attention has been given to debt and twin and triple crises, they are associated with higher income inequality than banking and currency crises. We also show that the effects of banking crises on income inequality in low-income, lower-middle income and upper-middle income countries (LICs, LMICs and UMICs respectively) are more pronounced than in high-income countries (HICs).

Policy implications

Long- and medium-term plans for social assistance programmes in LICS, LMICs and UMICs are needed and should be flexible so that social safety nets can be rapidly adjusted to protect vulnerable groups. Maintaining a high level of fiscal balance during tranquil periods is critical to implementing welfare state expansion policies in times of crisis. Top marginal income and estate taxes should be raised to avoid very high concentrations of income in the aftermath of financial crises. The minimum wage should also be raised to protect low-income workers.

1 INTRODUCTION

Over a decade after the Great Recession of 2007/2008, rising income inequality has been a major concern of politicians, economists, and policy-makers. Evidence from the Great Recession shows that millions of people have lost their jobs, their homes, and their life savings. This has seen the living standards of the various population groups deteriorate. However, do financial crises affect everyone equally? Despite recent growing concern about income inequality, mixed empirical findings have given rise to a long-standing debate about the distribution of income in the aftermath of financial crises.

Financial crises tend to be associated with rising unemployment, slowdowns in economic activity, and welfare state retrenchment (Baldacci et al., 2002; Eurofound, 2017; Roine et al., 2009). This could be more harmful to the poor, who lack the ability to cover unexpected expenses (Galbraith & Jiaqing, 1999; Jenkins et al., 2012; Nguyen et al., 2021; OECD, 2013). Moreover, high-income earners at the top of the income distribution might be able to avoid the adverse effects of financial crises by exporting their capital ahead of the crash and seeing their debts written off (Honohan, 2005). In sharp contrast, Wolff (2013) argues that financial crises can narrow the income gap as earning assets of the rich can be substantially wiped out during periods of financial crises, whereas the poorwho do not hold corporate and non-corporate equityare less influenced by financial crises. Since the controversy remains about the connection between financial crises and income inequality, little is known about appropriate policies to be implemented to avoid rising income inequality in the aftermath of financial crises and the long run. For this reason, the question that this study aims to address is how financial crises affect the distribution of income.

One reason for the mixed empirical evidence in the existing literature might be the substantial variation across the studies with regard to methods and sample periods. This study departs from the insights of the literature on financial crises and income inequality in four fundamental ways. First, to provide a more comprehensive view regarding financial crises and income inequality, we use a large cross-country sample over the last six decades, whereas previous studies tend to focus on a specific country or limited groups of countries over a short period of time. Second, we employ an appropriate dynamic panel data model to address the potential reverse causality between financial crises and income inequality, which previous studiesby using comparative analysis or simple econometric methods (see, e.g., Baldacci et al., 2002; de Haan & Sturm, 2017)make no attempt to account for. Third, unlike existing studies that focus on banking crises, especially the Great Recession, we account for not only banking crises but also currency, debt, and twin and triple crises. Four, we explore the heterogeneous effects of financial crises on income inequality between low-income countries (LICs), lower-middle income countries (LMICs), upper-middle income countries (UMICs), and high-income countries (HICs), as most financial crises occur in non-high-income countries.

Results from our generalized method of moments (GMM) indicate that any type of financial crisis results in higher income inequality. Digging deeper into the types of financial crises, we find that debt and twin and triple crises are associated with a larger income gap than banking and currency crises. This emphasizes the need for academics and policy-makers to pay more attention to different types of financial crises rather than banking crises or major crisis events. Moreover, by splitting the sample between LICs, LMICs, UMICs and HICs, all types of financial crises are found to increase income inequality in LICs and LMICs/UMICs. Regarding HICs, only banking crises appear to increase the income gap, while currency, debt, and twin and triple crises do not emerge as important drivers of income inequality. What is striking is that while banking crises last longer and hit HICs deeper in terms of output losses, the impact of those crises on income inequality is more pronounced in LICs and LMICs/UMICs.

The rest of the article is organized in the following sections. Section 2 reviews the channels through which financial crises, in general, and their respective types affect income inequality. Section 3 describes the data and variables used. Section 4 presents the methodology. Section 5 discusses the main findings and additional analyses. Section 6 offers some concluding remarks together with policy implications.

2 LITERATURE REVIEW

Over the years, income inequality in the aftermath of financial crises has received increasing attention, but ambiguities remain. There is no consensus on the role that financial crises play as drivers of income inequality and the channels they work through. On the one hand, as the well-off tend to hold significant shares of corporate and non-corporate equity, financial crises can wipe out a large amount of their wealth (Wolff, 2013). By contrast, the poorwho do not have asset incomesare less directly influenced by falling asset prices in times of crisis (Baldacci et al., 2002). Moreover, some studies suggest that welfare state expansion programmes can cushion the effects of financial crises on income inequality as they are targeted towards the poor. For example, Piketty and Saez (2013) examines income inequality in the US during the Great Recession and find that income inequality fell sharply during the period 20082009, but it was followed by a strong rebound in 2010. They conclude that the crisis did not seem to influence the long-term trend in terms of top income shares. Grabka (2015) finds no evidence of significant income distribution changes during the Great Recession in Germany.

On the other hand, another strand of the literature suggests that elevated levels of income inequality following financial crises can be explained in three main ways. First, rising unemployment in the aftermath of financial crises could increase inequality as low-income earners are more likely to lose their jobs (Fiorio & Saget, 2010; Galbraith & Jiaqing, 1999). This happens because they are less skilled and tend to be employed in more cyclical industries (Hoynes et al., 2012). Moreover, due to the lack of secured job contracts with flexible wages and high firing costs, small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)which make up the largest share of the total employment in the economyhave more incentive to lay off their low-income workers during financial crises (Nguyen et al., 2021). For this reason, less experienced, less educated, and young workersthose from low-income groupshave a much higher chance of job loss and suffer more during a financial crisis (Baldacci et al., 2002; UNDP, 2020).1 For example, higher unemployment is the main driver of rising income inequality in the aftermath of the Great Recession in Europe and the US (Eurofound, 2017; Jenkins et al., 2012).

Second, while unemployment may have a direct effect on income inequality through the channels just described, it can indirectly increase income inequality by rising more wage inequality (Autor et al., 2017; Jenkins et al., 2012).2 In particular, the wage Phillips curve suggests that higher unemployment results in lower wage growth as it weakens the bargaining power of workers (Autor et al., 2017; Goda et al., 2017; Phillips, 1958). Thiscoupled with the reduction in work hours of lower earners during financial criseswould further depress the wages of low-income workers and then widens income inequality. The theory is consistent with the empirical evidence of larger wage inequality observed in the aftermath of the Great Depression and the recent Great Recession (see, e.g., Dauth et al., 2015; Jenkins et al., 2012). Moreover, financial crises are associated with job losses and a drop in the official market participation rate and lower demand for services in the informal sector, which is also the poorest segment of the vulnerable groups in the population. In this regard, the entry of unemployed formal-sector labour into the informal sector creates additional downward pressure on the earnings of informal workers (Baldacci et al., 2002; Colombo et al., 2019).

Third, public social spending cuts in the wake of financial crises could be a reason for the higher income inequality witnessed in their aftermath. To cushion the real effects of financial crises on the labour market, governments tend to boost demand and cushion falls in household disposal income by implementing various welfare expansion programmes. If their fiscal stimulus packages are sufficiently large, financial crises can lead to a narrowing of income inequality as the disposal income of low-income groups falls less than the market income as a result of government support by means of public cash transfers and personal income taxes, as seen in Iceland, Poland, and Portugal during the period 20072010 (Eurofound, 2017; OECD, 2013).

However, given the fact that tax revenues fall in times of crisis due to households' lower incomes, the government budget is often under a greater burden to finance their costly crisis mitigation policies and to cope with the surge in demand for unemployment benefits and social protection. Consequently, they may not be able to maintain their welfare expansion programmes due to the rapid build-up of public debt and fiscal deficit. This is more pronounced when their pre-crisis fiscal deficit and public debt are at unsustainable levels (Gasparini & Lustig, 2011).

In fact, many countries initially expanded their welfare programmes in the first years of the Great Recession but implemented welfare state reform in the aftermath, such as Iceland, Ireland, and Spain. This lays the ground for income inequality to rise (Roine et al., 2009). One explanation is that low-income individuals and households tend to be hit harder during periods of financial crises, while spending cuts reduce the volume of publicly provided critical social services and limit access to these services (Baldacci et al., 2002). This is in line with the cross-national study by Jenkins et al. (2012), which shows that household income distribution did not change much during the period 20072009 due to tremendous government support through the tax and benefit system. However, income distribution changed significantly after 2009 as a result of fiscal consolidation measures implemented for budgetary reasons in the aftermath of the Great Recession.

Episodic financial crises can be drivers of long-term income inequality as they can have permanent effects on unemployment, wage inequality, and cuts in social spending. For example, 10years after the Great Recession, the levels of employment in SMEs in European manufacturing industry have still not reached pre-crisis levels (European Commission, 2017). Moreover, it has been argued that financial crises lead to long-lasting cuts in public social spending (Nguyen et al., 2021), which in turn have long-term effects on income equality. Indeed, since children from low-income households face more difficulties in accessing education, the supply of skilled labour will be lower in the long run, which eventually increases the skill premium due to the increased demand for skilled workers (UNDP, 2020). This can also increase inequality permanently, and intergenerational mobility will fall as wealthy parents can buy their children educational advantages that low-income parents cannot afford.

These discussions imply that financial crises might be associated with a long-term increase in income inequality. This sheds light on Lustig and Legovim (2001), who found that the impact of financial crises on income inequality is highly persistent, and that subsequent growth cannot compensate for the widening income inequality created in the aftermath of financial crises. This means that financial crises not only have an immediate impact on income inequality but also lead to higher inequalities for future generations. Therefore, we posit that financial crises increase income inequality.

However, the strict focus on banking crises and the Great Recession is a shortcoming of the literature (see, e.g., de Haan & Sturm, 2017; Jenkins et al., 2012). Financial crises tend to be multifaceted, hence the effects of different types of financial crises on income inequality can be heterogeneous. As argued by Fiorio and Saget (2010), the effects of financial crises in terms of distribution vary across countries according to the types of financial crises considered. For example, Roine et al. (2009) find that banking crises significantly reduce top income shares, while the occurrence of currency crises does not. Apart from this reference, the literature on cross-country effects of financial crises on income inequality is fragmented, as previous studies tend to focus on only one type of financial crisis, and their results are mixed. A brief summary is provided below of the mechanisms through which different types of financial crises could influence income inequality.

Systemic banking crises are often associated with credit crunches and capital flights, which are particularly harmful to production and investment activities (Brown, 2013; Nguyen et al., 2022b). Consequently, the income gap will widen due to the increase in unemployment and wage inequality. Moreover, the direct costs of crisis interventions during periods of banking crises could be substantial. For example, costly interventions such as unlimited liquidity support and bailouts of failing financial institutions can rapidly deplete the government budget (Amaglobeli et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2022a), which can lead to drastic cuts in public social spending and subsequent higher income inequality. This lends support to de Haan and Sturm (2017), who provide cross-country evidence of a positive relationship between banking crises and income inequality. They argue that wealth and capital losses during banking crises may have a negative impact on the top income distribution, but that the poor are harder hit.

Currency crises are characterized by a sharp depreciation of the domestic currency, which might widen income inequality through their effects on the product market. Significant currency depreciation, in particular, increases the price of imported goods and hurts middle-income groups as durable consumption goods, especially in lower- and upper-middle income countries, are mainly imported. Low-income groups are hit even harder due to the rise in cost of tradable domestic goods, especially staple goods (Brown, 2013). Similarly, Baldacci et al. (2002) argue that currency depreciation has an impact on the price of imported food, which increases domestic food prices and the burden on low-income individuals and households that are net food consumers. This is consistent with the empirical evidence provided by Galbraith and Jiaqing (1999), who examine 34 currency crises during the period 19721995, finding that currency crises increase income inequality as lower-wage workers are more vulnerable to changes in the product market.

While governments can expand their countries' welfare state to tackle the adverse effects of financial crises on the poor during banking and currency crises, the fiscal position of countries in a debt crisis forces them to implement drastic welfare state reforms, including painful cuts in their public services (Brown, 2013; Nguyen et al., 2021), which are evident from the waves of debt crises in Latin American countries during the 1980s. Moreover, debt crises last, on average, much longer than banking and currency crises (Nguyen et al., 2022a), which implies that their associated spending cuts could be more prolonged. This hurts low-income groupswho are more dependent on government supports in times of crisisand reduces the human capital of the poor and then their ability to grow out of poverty (Gasparini & Lustig, 2011).

Besides the three types of financial crises, it is worth noting that financial crises are not single events. In practice, they tend to overlap or run on from each other. Thus, we account for twin and triple crises, which are formed by various combinations between banking, currency, and debt crises. The effects of twin and triple crises on income inequality have received little attention in the literature, even though they can result in far more severe consequences on income equality. One possible reason is that twin and triple crises are more harmful to human and social wellbeing. In particular, Nguyen et al. (2021) find that the impact of twin and triple crises on education, health, and income is more severe than single-event financial crises. Moreover, Cerra and Saxena (2017) examine the consequences of financial crises in 190 countries and find that, while currency and banking crises result in permanent loss in output of 5% and 10% on average, respectively, the figure for twin crises is 15%.

3.1 Financial crisis identification

In this study, crises variables are dummies that take the values of 1 for crisis episodes, and 0 otherwise. To identify financial crises, we rely on the crises database by Nguyen et al. (2022a). In particular, information for systemic banking crises episodes during the period 19702017 is provided by Laeven and Valencia (2020). Under their definition, a banking crisis is dated when a government implements at least three out of six significant policy interventions.3 We then use the crises database by Nguyen et al. (2022a), which extends Laeven and Valencia's (2020) database to the period 19702019.

Following Frankel and Rose (1996), Nguyen et al. (2022a) define a currency crisis when a year-on-year depreciation of a domestic currency vis vis the US dollar is of at least 30%, and this depreciation is at least 10 percentage points higher than the previous year depreciation. Nguyen et al. (2022a) use both end-of-period and average exchange rates to avoid identify a currency crisis too late or early, as can be observed in the previous studies (see, e.g., Frankel & Rose, 1996; Laeven & Valencia, 2020).

As for debt crises identification, using the Credit Rating Assessment Group (CRAG) database of sovereign defaults (see Beers & Mavalwalla, 2018), Nguyen et al. (2022a) define a sovereign debt crisis as episodes during which total sovereign defaults exceed 1% of GDP in at least three consecutive years or when total sovereign defaults exceed 7% of GDP. Using the CRAG database allows Nguyen et al. (2022a) to identify many unnoticed debt crises, which were ignored in such previous studies as Balteanu and Erce (2018) and Laeven and Valencia (2020).4

Twin and triple crises are dated using a one-year length interval (see Laeven & Valencia, 2020; Nguyen et al., 2022a). Employing the crises databases by Nguyen et al. (2022a), our crises database includes 151 banking crises (19702019), 385 currency crises (19602019), 200 sovereign debt crises (19602019), and 96 twin and triple crises (19702019). Further details of our crises database are summarised in Table 3.

3.2 Income inequality

Income inequality is measured by the Gini coefficient based on the households' (pre-tax and pre-transfer) income from the Standardized World Income Inequality Database (SWIID). Using income before taxes allows separating the Gini coefficient from the influence of the redistribution policies via taxes and transfers. SWIID is the most comprehensive database of income inequality (de Haan & Sturm, 2017; Delis et al., 2014). The Gini coefficient is derived from the Lorenz curve and ranges between 0 and 100, with 0 representing perfect equality (every resident has the same income) and 100 representing perfect inequality (one resident earned all the income). As a check for robustness, we use the income share of the top decile of the population and the poverty gap at USD 1.90 a day as alternative measures of income inequality.

3.3 Control variables

A number of macroeconomic, institutional, and demographic control variables are included in our regressions to account for the effects of these controls and isolate the specific impact of financial crises variables on income inequality. Our choices of control variables were based on the extensive literature on the determinants of income inequality and the completeness of data coverage.

First, we control for trade openness as the standard HeckscherOhlin theory in international trade suggests that trade openness increases income redistribution. The log of gross domestic product (GDP) per capita is included to control for the income inequality dynamics along the development process. The rich could benefit from growth more than the rest of the population because they are capital owners and top executives (Piketty & Saez, 2003; Ronie et al., 2009). In line with the Kuznets hypothesis, we also include the square of the log of GDP per capita to control for the nonlinear effect of economic development on income distribution (Nikoloski, 2012). Government spending constitutes another macroeconomic control. Larger government spending can effectively narrow the income gap if used efficiently (Canare et al., 2020; Delis et al., 2014).

Second, we account for the effects of democracy as democratic regimes provide more power to the middle- and low-income groups, who hold a majority of votes. Politicians aiming to compete for those votes are more likely to respond to their citizens' redistributive concerns (Brown & Hunter, 2004; Gerring et al., 2012; Nguyen et al., 2021).

Finally, we control for the effects of education on income inequality. Greater access to education is expected to lower the income gap by allowing a larger share of the population to be engaged in high-skill activities (Jaumotte et al., 2013; Rajan, 2010). Moreover, population size is included to control for the demographics in each country (Delis et al., 2014).

4 METHODS

To estimate the effects of different types of financial crises on income inequality, we consider the following dynamic panel data model:
...Inequa.li,t=i+Inequa+.li,t1+.FinCrisisi,t+.Xi,t1+t+.i,t(1)
where subscripts i and t denote countries and years, respectively; Inequal refers to the vector of income inequality variable; FinCrisis represents the vector of coefficients associated with the different types of financial crises; X is the set of control variables; i captures country-specific effects; and t represents time-specific effects, which are captured by a trend variable5; .i,t is the usual error terms; , , and , are the respective coefficient vectors.

We employ this dynamic specification to account for the persistence in income inequality (Delis et al., 2014). The method also allows controlling for the possible reverse causality that exists between income inequality and financial crisis variables. In fact, there is growing theoretical and empirical evidence that income inequality is a potential driver of financial crises (see, e.g., Kirschenmann et al., 2016; Perugini et al., 2016; Rajan, 2010). Moreover, a dynamic specification is necessary to address the potential causal relationships between explanatory variables. For example, trade openness can increase the probability of a banking crisis (Nguyen et al., 2022b).

The unobservable country-specific effects (i) in Eq. (1) can be captured by the fixed- or random-effects models. However, the inclusion of the lagged dependent variable in these models is not unproblematic as it results in severely biased coefficients due to the correlation between the lagged dependent variable and unobservable fixed effectsthe so-called Nickell bias (Nickell, 1981). Hence, instrumental variables methods, especially those relying on GMM specifications, are employed to address the bias. Those methods also better fit our panel data structure as the number of countries considered (128) is higher than the time dimension (19602019).

The application of GMM allows using lagged values of explanatory variables as valid instruments (Bermpei et al., 2018; Nguyen, 2021). In our models, however, treating lagged values of these variables as additional instruments may give rise to the instrument proliferation issue. In particular, due to the limited number of countries considered, the number of instruments can become larger than the number of cross-sectional observations, as the more variables being treated as endogenous, the greater the number of instruments employed. Moreover, since we consider several potential explanatory variables for income inequality over the last six decades, finding credible instruments is not feasible for all variables. Thus, borrowing from Delis et al. (2014), we only treat the lagged dependent variable as endogenous. We attempted to reduce endogeneity issues and simultaneity bias by using lagged values of all control variables. As suggested by Roodman (2009), we use collapsed instruments to further reduce the number of instruments.

Since we assume weak exogeneity of the explanatory variables, future unanticipated shocks to Gini coefficients should not affect the current values of the explanatory variables. This assumption is justified by Hansen tests for the overall validity of all instruments, which never reject the null of exogenous instruments. Difference-in-Hansen tests also confirm the validity of the subsets of instruments. Moreover, the ArellanoBond autocorrelation tests also support the validity of the instruments used. Thus, it may be safe to assume that the GMM results presented in this study are valid (Arellano & Bond, 1991).6

Results from all specifications indicate that any type of financial crisis increases income inequality.7 Compared to other types of financial crises, debt crises appear to be associated with a larger income gap. In particular, the occurrence of debt crises is equivalent to an 0.258% increase in the Gini coefficient, ceteris paribus. One explanation is that governments are often compelled to implement drastic welfare state retrenchment to lower budget deficit and public debt during a debt crisis. Consequently, low-income groups suffer more from public social spending cuts. An alternative explanation is that debt crisis, on average, lasts longer than other types of financial crises (see Table 1). In this regard, their impact on income inequality is more pronounced.

Besides, the coefficient of the Banking crisis variable is a surprise. It shows that the effects of banking crises on income inequality are less severe than other types of financial crises, which indicates that the focus of previous studies on banking crisesneglecting other types of financial crisesprovides an incomplete picture of the consequences of financial crises on income equality. Instead, special attention should be given to debt and twin and triple crises as they are more harmful to income equality.

Regarding the results of controllers, increased government expenditure appears to increase income inequality, possibly reflecting that government spending policy might not well target the lower-income groups (Delis et al., 2014). The log of GDP per capita and its square confirm the Kuznets hypothesis on the U-curve relationship between economic development and income inequality. As expected, education helps to reduce income inequality. However, trade openness and population size do not emerge as important drivers of income inequality, which is consistent with Roine et al. (2009) and Delis et al. (2014).

To check for the robustness of the main findings on financial crises and income inequality, we use alternative measures of income inequality: the income share held by the top decile of the population and the poverty gap at USD 1.90 a day. As pointed out by Kirschenmann et al. (2016) and Alvaredo et al. (2018), the calculations of synthetic indices like the Gini index could be inaccurate due to the quality of country-specific data. In particular, the calculations of Gini index might be influenced by systematic and random data errors such as inconsistencies and anomalies. Using income share and the poverty gap can address these problems.

Table 5 reports the effects of financial crises on income inequality, proxied by the income share held by the highest 10% of the income distribution and the poverty gap at USD 1.90 a day.8 The results in all specifications indicate that all types of financial crises increase the income share of the top decile and widen the poverty gap. These findings further confirm that financial crises generally increase income inequality. The coefficients of debt and twin and triple crises again reinforce our conclusion that these types of crises have stronger effects on income inequality than banking and currency crises. The results of control variables are also consistent with the findings presented so far.

5.2 Financial crises and income inequality: A deeper look at LICs, LMICs/UMICs and HICs

While we have witnessed financial crises in most countries in the world over the last six decades, most financial crises occurred in LICs and LMICs/UMICs. Indeed, our crisis database shows that more than 92% of financial crises occurred in those countries over the period 19602019 (see Table 3). More specifically, around 77.4% of banking crises, 94.5% of currency crises, 96% of debt crises, and approximately 97% of twin and triple crises were identified in LICs and LMICs/UMICs countries. This implies that the findings presented in Table 4 might not provide a complete picture of the effects of different types of financial crises on income inequality as currency, debt, and twin and triple crises are phenomena of LICs and LMICs/UMICs countries.

Nevertheless, LICs and LMICs/UMICs might experience less severe and shorter financial crises, while HICs could face substantial output losses in the aftermath of financial crises (Brown, 2013; Laeven & Valencia, 2020). This means that low-income groups in HICs could suffer more, and the income gap could be wider in those countries. One explanation for these heterogeneous effects is the substantially different economic conditions between different income groups of countries in times of crisis (Nguyen et al., 2022a).

Thus, to offer important insights into the relationship between financial crises and income inequality, it is important to unveil the heterogeneous effects of financial crises on income inequality between different income groups of countries. In doing so, we split our sample into LICs, LMICs/UMICs and HICs.9 In specifications (1)(5) of Table 6, the results show that all types of financial crises considered increase income inequality in LICs and LMICs/UMICs, which are consistent with our findings presented in Table 4. More attention should also be paid to twin and triple crises as they are associated with a wider income gap than other types of financial crises.

As for HICs, banking crises and financial crises, in general, are found to increase income inequality, while the effects of currency, debt, and twin and triple crises on income inequality are statistically insignificant. This can be explained by the fact that currency, debt, and twin and triple crises rarely occur in HICs.

However, it is surprising that the impact of banking crises on income inequality is more pronounced in LICs and LMICs/UMICs, although banking crises last longer and hit HICs deeper in terms of output losses (Brown, 2013; Laeven & Valencia, 2020). This could be because HICs tend to protect their welfare state more aggressively during financial crises (Kiendrebeogo et al., 2017; Mohseni-Cheraghlou, 2016). Thus, financial crises become less harmful to the poor in those countries. In contrast, LICs and LMICs/UMICs are more likely to curtail social spending in the aftermath of financial crises due to rising budget deficit and public debt (Conceio et al., 2011; Nguyen et al., 2021).

5.3 The role of political factors

Given that political factors are important in terms of determining the ability to implement social policies and their orientations of ruling governments, we consider the role of a few political factors that may influence income inequality in LICs and LMICs/UMICs and HICs in Table 7.10 First, we examine the effects of political ideology on income inequality. Governments are heterogeneous as reflected by opposite orientations of social policies between the left- and right-wing governments. Left-wing governments are more likely to favour higher social expenditure and generous and redistributive welfare states because they represent the middle-income and low-income voters, who are more dependent on welfare programmes (Castro & Martins, 2020; Delis et al., 2014; Garrett & Mitchell, 2001). By contrast, right-wing governments representing middle-income and high-income voters tend to favour balanced budgets and lean welfare states (Castro & Martins, 2020; McManus, 2019; Nguyen et al., 2021). Thus, right-wing governments might be associated with higher income inequality. In this analysis, Right-wing government and centre government are added to compare with left-wing governments. As expected, results from all specifications of Table 7 indicate that right-wing governments widen the income gap in both groups of countries, which is also consistent with Delis et al. (2014), who find that left-leaning governments reduce income inequality.

Second, political instability might result in higher income inequality by producing little programmatic cohesion of social policies. This happens because incumbent governments do not expect to reap benefits from long-term social policies as they are uncertain about their future reappointments (Gyimah-Brempong & Muoz de Camacho, 1998; Jens, 2017). Instead, they tend to pursue short-term policies at the expense of long-term development (Gerring et al., 2012; Keefer, 2007). In this regard, economic agents are also less likely to invest in human capital formation such as education, training, skills, and healthcare because they are not sure about the capabilities to benefit from such investment (Aisen & Veiga, 2013; Gyimah-Brempong & Muoz de Camacho, 1998). Consequently, the income gap between the rich and the poor could be larger since most people are less engaged in highly skilled activities and quality healthcare services. Indeed, results from Table 7 show that the political instability variable has a positive and significant effect on income inequality in both groups of countries.

Third, another political variable of interest is the election cycle. Castro and Martins (2018) argue that politicians may implement welfare expansion programmes to achieve more votes when elections are tight. This should have a positive effect on income equality as more resources are allocated for welfare state expansion. Some studies also provide empirical evidence of incumbents' opportunistic behaviour in increasing spending on public services such as healthcare and social protection in election years (see, e.g., Potrafke, 2010; Shi & Svensson, 2006). To check for the effects of the election cycle, we added a dummy variable for election years into our models. Nevertheless, results from Table 7 shows that the election cycle variable does not reveal itself as an important driver of income inequality in both groups of countries. Perhaps, governments might implement contraction measures to address imbalances generated to produce that short-term opportunistic behaviour.

In summary, despite the inclusion of political variables and a shorter examined period (19762019), our results remain consistent: all types of financial crises have a positive impact on income inequality in LICs and LMICs/UMICs. The results are also robust when we use a full sample analysis with both groups of countries.11 Again, only banking crises appear to increase income inequality in HICs, and their effects are less pronounced than those of LICs and LMICs/UMICs. We tried to interact political variables with financial crises variables to explore how political factors influence the effects of financial crises on income inequality. However, the interaction terms render the effects of political factors insignificant as they do not add any information to our models, while they produce disturbance due to redundant information.

6 CONCLUSION

The primary aim of this study is to examine the link between financial crises and income inequality using a cross-country sample looking at the last six decades. We pay attention to income inequality as there is no consensus on whether income inequality widens or narrows in the aftermath of financial crises.

Our results indicate that all types of financial crises widen the income gap between the rich and the poor. Special attention should be paid to debt and twin and triple crises as they are associated with a larger income gap than banking and currency crises. A similar conclusion can be reached regarding the effects of debt and twin and triple crises on the poverty gap at USD 1.90 a day and the income share of the top decile of the population. By focusing on the heterogeneous effects of different types of financial crises on income inequality between low-, middle-income countries and high-income countries, we find that all types of financial crises increase income inequality in low- and middle-income countries, whereas only banking crises drive higher income inequality in high-income countries. We also find that the impact of banking crises on income inequality is more severe in low- and middle-income countries, even though banking crises tend to be longer and deeper in high-income countries.

As the income gap between the rich and the poor is widened in the aftermath of any type of financial crisis in both groups of countries, proper government interventions are necessary to narrow the gap. In particular, implementing credible fiscal plans in the wake of a financial crisis can be useful to prevent low-income groups from falling into poverty and to mitigate distribution effects. For example, expanding social safety nets to the most vulnerable groups via cash transfers is needed to protect pro-poor spending. This also helps to improve the confidence of investors and avoid a plunge in employment.

Education and health budgets should be protected during financial crises to avoid leaving long-lasting consequences on human capital, which further increase the income gap in the long run. Moreover, the minimum wage should also be raised to protect low-income workers.

Nevertheless, social policies in times of crisisespecially in regards to LICs, LMICS/UMICstend to be inefficient and do not deliver fully on their objectives due to political purposes, budget constraints, poor institutional quality, and inefficiencies in the administrative systems (Delis et al., 2014; Nguyen et al., 2021; Vtyurina, 2020;). Thus, the main challenge facing those countries is shaping appropriate policies during non-crisis periods to mitigate the impact of future financial crises on income inequality.

First, long- and medium-term plans for credible social assistance programmes in low- and middle-income countries are needed. They should be flexible so that social safety nets can be rapidly adjusted to protect vulnerable groups. Second, maintaining a high level of fiscal balance during tranquil periods is critical to financing welfare state expansion policies during periods of financial crises. In fact, most low- and middle-income countries cut their social spending and other social assistance programmes for budgetary reasons during financial crises (Atkinson & Morelli, 2011; Nguyen et al., 2021). This is the driving force behind the rise in income inequality associated with banking crises in low- and middle-income countries. Third, in line with Piketty and Saez (2013) and Goda et al. (2017), we suggest that top marginal income and estate taxes should be raised to avoid very high concentrations of income in the aftermath of financial crises. Fourth and finally, the minimum wage should also be raised to protect low-income workers.
